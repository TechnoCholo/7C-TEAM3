FUNCTION OF BONES

🦠

Christian P, Mateo G, Isabella P, and Joshua C

Bones provide structural support, protect vital organs, enable movement, store essential minerals, and host bone marrow. Far from just being a rigid frame, they are active, living organs that actively manage your body's overall metabolism.

Support:

They provide a scaffold to hold the body up, giving shape and stability.

Protection:

Rigid bones encase soft, delicate organs to protect them from impact or injury (e.g., the skull protects the brain, and the rib cage protects the heart).

Movement:

Bones act as levers that work with muscles, tendons, and ligaments to allow you to walk, run, and twist.

Mineral and Energy Storage:

Bones store essential minerals needed for health.

DISEASES /INJURIES

🦠

Christian P, Mateo G, Isabella P, and Joshua C

Bone diseases and injuries can be divided into degenerative, genetic, infectious, and traumatic categories. These conditions can range from bone-weakening disorders like osteoporosis to physical fractures—directly impact bone strength, structure, and density, often causing severe pain and mobility issues.

Metabolic & Degenerative Diseases:

  • • Osteoporosis
  • • Osteopenia
  • • Paget's Disease of Bone
  • • Osteomalacia & Rickets

Genetic & Rare Disorders:

  • • Osteogenesis Imperfecta
  • • Osteopetrosis

Infections & Vascular Diseases:

  • • Osteomyelitis
  • • Osteonecrosis (Avascular Necrosis)

Bone Cancers & Tumors:

  • • Osteosarcoma
  • • Metastatic Bone Disease

Traumatic Injuries (Fractures):

  • • Stress Fracture
  • • Comminuted Fracture
  • • Greenstick Fracture
  • • Open Fracture

PARTS OF THE BONE

🦠

Christian P, Mateo G, Isabella P, and Joshua C

In the bone we have 6 parts: the compact bone, spongy bone, red bone marrow, yellow bone marrow, arteries, and the veins.

Compact Bone:

Dense outer shell made of repeating units called osteons. It bears weight and resists mechanical stress.

Spongy Bone:

Light internal network of bone arches called trabeculae. It absorbs impacts and houses marrow.

Red Bone Marrow:

Highly vascular tissue that produces red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.

Yellow Bone Marrow:

Fat-rich tissue that replaces red marrow in adults and acts as an energy reserve.

Arteries:

Blood vessels entering via nutrient foramina to supply oxygen and nutrients to bone cells.

Veins:

Blood vessels that carry metabolic waste away from the bone and transport newly formed blood cells.

TYPES OF BONES

🦠

Christian P, Mateo G, Isabella P, and Joshua C

Bones come in different shapes and sizes, each designed for specific purposes in your body:

Long Bones:

Longer than they are wide. They act as levers to facilitate movement. Examples: Femur (thigh bone), humerus (upper arm), and tibia (shin bone).

Short Bones:

Roughly cube-shaped, providing stability and support with limited movement. Examples: Carpals (wrist bones) and tarsals (ankle bones).

Flat Bones:

Thin, flattened, and usually curved. They protect vital internal organs. Examples: Skull bones, ribs, and the sternum (breastbone).

Irregular Bones:

Complex shapes that do not fit into other categories. Examples: Vertebrae (spine) and the pelvis.

Sesamoid Bones:

Small, round bones embedded within tendons to protect them from stress and wear.

FUN FACTS

🦠

Christian P, Mateo G, Isabella P, and Joshua C

Discover amazing facts about your skeleton:

• Babies start with more: You have 206 bones in your adult body, but you were born with around 300. They are separated by soft cartilage to make birth easier, and fuse together as you grow.
• Once for ounce, they beat steel: Compact bone is incredibly dense and can support heavy loads without snapping.
• The "bone" that isn't: When you bang your elbow and feel a weird, zapping tingling pain, you are hitting your ulnar nerve—not your "funny bone" (which is actually your upper arm bone, the humerus).
• Bone Floater: The hyoid bone in your throat is the only bone in the entire body that doesn't connect to any other bone. It just floats there to support your tongue and help you speak.
• A brand new you: Your skeleton is constantly breaking down and rebuilding itself. Thanks to these amazing cells, you get a "brand new" skeleton every 10 years.
